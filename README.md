# CS348K Project

Choosing the right representation for a given task is a central theme in computer graphics. A simple example is the distinction between implicit and explicit representations of 3D scenes. Implicit representations, such as algebraic surfaces or signed distance functions (SDFs), are well-suited for inside/outside queries but are less convenient for sampling surface points. In contrast, explicit representations like meshes or point clouds allow direct surface sampling but can be less flexible for certain geometric queries.

In this project, we focus on **novel view synthesis from multi-view photographs**, where a 3D scene representation is learned through gradient-based optimization. Recently, Gaussian Splatting has emerged as a state-of-the-art approach due to its ease of optimization, high rendering quality, and real-time performance. However, a key limitation of this representation is its relatively high storage cost. Because it is explicit, a moderately sized scene may require millions of Gaussian primitives, each parameterized by dozens of values. This large primitive count is partly driven by the need to represent high-frequency appearance details: since each Gaussian carries only a limited color representation (e.g., spherical harmonics), many primitives are required in visually complex regions.

To address this limitation, recent work has explored incorporating texture information into Gaussian splatting. Most approaches extend each Gaussian with its own texture map, increasing expressiveness but also significantly increasing storage requirements. In contrast, a recent method called Nexels proposes to decouple geometry and appearance: geometry is represented by Gaussian splats, while appearance is modeled by a shared neural network. This design allows for more compact representations while maintaining high visual quality.

This idea aligns with our central objective: **identifying representations for novel view synthesis that achieve the best visual quality under a fixed storage budget.** We measure storage in terms of model size (e.g., MB), and evaluate quality using both standard image metrics and human perceptual judgments. While training and rendering speed are not primary goals, more compact representations may naturally offer efficiency benefits.

Building on the Nexels framework, we propose to investigate two open questions:

**Representation efficiency under equal storage constraints.** Instant-NGP is a compact and highly effective neural representation that was considered state-of-the-art prior to Gaussian splatting. In Nexels, Instant-NGP is used only for modeling appearance, while geometry remains explicit. We ask: given the same storage budget, how does Nexels compare to a fully neural representation such as Instant-NGP in terms of final rendering quality?

**Perceptual alignment of training and evaluation.** Current pipelines typically optimize simple reconstruction losses (e.g., L1, L2, MS-SSIM) and evaluate using image-based metrics such as PSNR, MS-SSIM, and LPIPS. However, rendered 3D scenes are ultimately consumed by humans. We propose to explore alternative loss functions inspired by perceptual similarity, such as Wasserstein-based losses used in learned image compression, to determine whether they improve visual quality. Furthermore, we aim to move beyond static image evaluation by conducting a human-subject study, where participants interactively explore rendered scenes and rate their perceived quality. This allows evaluation in a setting that more closely reflects real-world usage.
